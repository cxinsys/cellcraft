Combining scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq data

Single-cell transcriptomics has revolutionized our ability to describe the characteristics of cells, but it's important to note that simply identifying clusters is not enough for a deep biological understanding. Integrating datasets from different cellular modalities presents a significant analytical challenge in gaining a better understanding of cellular identity and function. For instance, users might want to annotate both scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq datasets consistently when conducting experiments on the same biological system. There have been many attempts to gain deeper biological understanding by integrating scATAC-seq and scRNA-seq data. However, there are significant difficulties in integrating the two data. The reason is that in the case of scATAC-seq dataset analysis, the availability of genetic data at the single-cell level is limited, and in the case of scRNA-seq, there are not enough gene markers that can be easily interpreted.

To overcome these difficulties in data integration, the paper Stuar**, Butler** et al(2019) presented a method. Based on the technique presented in the paper, in this guide we will perform the process of integrating data from scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq. To combine scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq data, several essential steps are required. These steps include annotating scATAC-seq cell data using scRNA-seq annotation data, visualizing and presenting the integrated data, leveraging scRNA-seq data to generate UMAP results for scATAC-seq.

In this tutorial, we will be treating approximately 12,000 human PBMC cell data provided by 10X Genomics. These data are generated using 10X Genomics' single cell multiome method, where data for both ATAC-seq and RNA-seq are simultaneously generated from the same individual cells.

By combining the data from scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq, we can more precisely distinguish cell types and cell states, uncover the factors responsible for differential gene expression, and also identify cells that share similar gene transcription patterns but exhibit differing chromatin landscapes.

2. Finding anchors between scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq datasets¶

Retrun to Index

To combine scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq datasets, we need to find anchors. To find anchors means to identify and match pairs of cells in cross-dataset that are in a matched biological state. In order to find these anchors, we assess the activity of each gene in terms of transcription. We start by measuring ATAC-seq counts within the 2 kb upstream region and gene body, using the GeneActivity()</strong> function in the Signac package. Subsequently, we utilize the gene activity scores obtained from scATAC-seq results in conjunction with gene expression data from scRNA-seq for Canonical Correlation Analysis. We apply this process to all genes that exhibit significant variability in the scRNA-seq dataset.
